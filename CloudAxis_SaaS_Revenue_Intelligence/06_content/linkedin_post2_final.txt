Something shifted in how I think about AI and professional work.

Not what I expected when I started.

─────────────────────────────

The common fear was that AI would replace expertise.

What I actually found: it exposed how much of what we called "expertise" was really just execution.

The hours spent building models. Formatting disclosures. Writing formulas. Structuring roll-forwards. That work required skill — but not judgment. AI handles it now. Faster and more consistently than a person.

What's left is the part that was always the real job.

Deciding if the output is right for this organisation.
For this industry.
For this auditor.
For this moment.

That hasn't been automated. It's just become more visible.

─────────────────────────────

Here's what actually changed in practice:

Before — a small finance team couldn't produce what a large one could. Not because of knowledge. Because of time and resources.

Now — one person who knows what they're doing can produce what previously required a team.

The ceiling for smaller organisations just got significantly higher.

But the floor got more dangerous too.

A tool that produces wrong answers at scale — consistently, confidently, without flagging them — is worse than no tool at all. The output looks right. The math checks out. And the answer is still wrong for your context.

That's the risk nobody is talking about clearly enough.

─────────────────────────────

The skill that matters now isn't knowing how to use the tool.

It's knowing enough to govern it.

To recognise when the output is wrong.
To understand why.
To correct it.
And to know which decisions the tool cannot make, no matter how capable it gets.

That judgment is not a feature you can add later.
It has to already be there.

─────────────────────────────

I'm still figuring out what this means long term.

But I'm increasingly convinced that the professionals who will do well in this environment aren't the ones who resist these tools — or the ones who trust them blindly.

It's the ones who develop the judgment to know the difference.
